Gift of Winter

Being nocturnal, it's rare for Siberian flying squirrels to be active during bright daytime hours in February in the middle of winter. In order to capture this rare opportunity, a great deal of time was spent searching for a location. The Siberian flying squirrel is small, and it glides very quickly, so catching one on camera is very difficult, but success was achieved here through a thorough understanding of the animal's movements, and determining a specific point to aim for in its flight. I feel that the animal's gliding motion and the surrounding area have been very gracefully captured in a single frame here.

Small Waterfall in the Forest

“Pa-Ngam-Ngon” waterfall is a small waterfall located at “Khun Dan Prakan Chon Dam” within Nakhon-Nayok province, Thailand (northeast of Bangkok). The waterfall is surrounded by clear water from the dam, and lush, pristine rain forest. I have chosen the month of November to be the perfect time to capture this image, it is when the jungle and waterfall is most alive. The only way to reach the waterfall is by a 20 minute boat ride, departing from the dam’s ridge, then a hike through 700-800 meters of jungle terrain.

The stunning waterfall flows down from a high cliff, initially I was stunned by the beauty of the waterfall and stood in bewilderment for a moment, absorbing my surroundings before I proceed to raise my camera and capture the moment. What I wanted to achieve in this image, is to capture the richness and abundances of the surrounding rainforest. I started scouring the area for the best angle, until I found a fallen tree trunk surrounded by moss and fern covered rocks, I then decided to use the fallen trunk and rocks as foreground and utilized the trunk as a line of sight to the waterfall.

To capture the perfect image I waited for clouds to block out the sun to reduce lens flare, and continued to mount my DSLR full frame Cannon 6D on a tripod. I chose the EF 17-40 mm f/4L USM lens and 17 mm distance to capture the lushness of the surrounding jungle, I also utilize CPL filter to reduce light reflection on leaves and waterfall. I set my F-stop to f/16 to create a deep depth of field through-out the image. With the exposure time set at 8 seconds and ISO speed at 100, I was able to capture the flow of the waterfall while leaves and surroundings remain still. Due to a low light environment at the time this image was taken my camera lighting calculations are “off” which result in over exposure. I then chose to set the Exposure bias to -0.7 to counteract and find the balance between light and dark.

Other than a lens hood on my camera, I also used my hand to block out any additional lens flare, and when the clouds drifts across the sun, a small breeze flowing through the lush jungle, I knew that this is my golden moment, then……Snap!

Squid

Squid are very active at night. I enjoy watching squid wriggle their tentacles into slithering spirals while hovering, and thrust them straight out like a bouquet of tails to swim. Squid can darken and lighten their pigmentation at will, an ability which becomes obvious when they are illuminated by a night diver's light.

This photo was taken in the Waigeo island of Raja Ampat, which is on the northwest tip of the Bird’s Head Peninsula of West Papua. Raja Ampat translates into English as Four Kings and gets its name from a local mythology about four kings that occupy the four big islands; Salawati, Batanta, Waigeo and Misool.

It is easy to find squid in Raja Ampat. Squid generally hold in mid-water, especially active at night, looking up and down for prey.

This squid was encountered at 5 meters depth and displayed its deadly fangs and claws trying to scare me. It is very difficult to focus in these conditions. I tried many shots, but this was the only successful one.

Helping Hands

We the Homo sapiens boast of our civilization and culture and in this pride we tend to forget, that there is this tiny kingdom of weaver ants which has a thriving colony which is full of life. They are predominant members of forest insect communities and found throughout the entire tropical world except America and belong to a relatively old genus Oecophylla in the subfamily Formicinae of hexapod order Hymenoptera with 11 fossil species reported from Oligocene and Miocene periods and two extant species; Oecophylla longinoda occurring widely across Afrotropical region and Oecophylla smaragdina is found in the Oriental, Palaearctic and Australian regions. Oecophylla smaragdina was diverged between 3.6 and 7.8 million years ago somewhere in China.

These ants exhibit marvelous architecture and intelligence to build their nest with leaves. When a new nest is required, individual workers scout for suitable clusters of leaves, which they grab with their mandibles and attempt to draw together.

Meanwhile other workers made their attention to the site and presumably by the success of the first workers and join the effort. To join the two leaves, ants form chains by holding each other’s waists and thus pull the leaves together as is depicted in the picture. In the image one ant is helping another in turning the leaf in suitable position so as to fit the requirement. The ants make their nests with the leaves of the tree sewn together. Subsequently a number of leaves were stretched in to position for binding, each held in place by rows of workers. As soon as all the leaves were brought together the process of stitching of these leaves commenced and the mature and experienced workers capture white colored and transparent late instar larvae with quite delicacy in their mandibles and move them to and fro on the edges of young tender leaves. In response to this movement or tingling feeling, the larvae release sticky material from their glands and a white sheet of silk joins the leaves one by one. This behavioral characteristic of ‘stitching or weaving leaves’ for nest formation is unique among these ants and the reason behind their name ‘Weaver Ants’.

Darter at Sunset

It was my 4th day in Bharatpur during last year’s winter and I was wondering if I could get the shot that I have been trying to get for the last two years. I was trying to get a shot with a darter spreading its wings with an orange sunset background. The darters or snakebirds are mainly tropical water birds and have long thin necks, which have a snake-like appearance. To dry their feathers after diving, darters move to a safe location (generally a nearby tree) and spread their wings. I was positioned right where I could see the sun going down and there was a tree where Darters usually come to dry their wings. After waiting for 15-20 minutes, the sun was getting very low and there were no signs of any Darters. Suddenly I saw Darter coming out from water and sit on the lowest branch of the tree. The Darter was still not in line with sun and I was hoping that she would move up by 4-5 feet so that I could frame the correctly. After waiting for a few more seconds the Darter moved up and came to the top of the tree. But at the same time, the sun had already gone down and this was the best shot I could get. Getting such shots is always challenging as we have no controls over such situations and there is a lot of waiting involved.

I Will Bite You

Greater Flamingos are social birds, like all flamingos. Their flocks can be very large and numerous. The great number of flamingos residing in a single area leaves room for aggressive behaviour between the members of the flock. Sometimes Greater Flamingo males fight over nest grounds or mates. The males can use their bill to fight or they have been observed chasing each other in the wild. They have other mechanisms of protecting and threatening other small birds. Sometimes due to space reasons they bite each other to go away. I found these flamingos in my hometown lake and was fortunate to capture their movements and aggressive calls so perfectly.
